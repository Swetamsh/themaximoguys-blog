## Post 1
The Maximo developer identity crisis is real.

You spent 10+ years mastering 7.6. Now MAS 9 changes the rules. But here's what nobody talks about: the shift looks completely different depending on your role.

## Post 2
**The App Developer**

You lived in automation scripts and custom MBOs. Java classes that extended MboSet. You knew MAXATTRIBUTE like the back of your hand.

In MAS? Your scripts mostly survive. Your Java needs a hard look. And GraphQL becomes your new best friend.

## Post 3
**The Integration Developer**

You were the MIF master. Publish channels, enterprise services, CRON tasks, and -- let's be honest -- more SOAP web services than anyone should have to deal with.

In MAS? MIF still exists. It's just not the center of the universe anymore.

## Post 4
**The Admin**

WebSphere whisperer. JVM tuning, connection pools, thread dumps at 3 AM.

In MAS? Your job transforms, not disappears. The tools change completely. But someone still needs to keep things running -- and your deep operational knowledge makes you the best candidate for that.

## Post 5
The common thread for all three:

- API-first thinking (no more direct SQL)
- Event-driven architecture (not batch-first)
- Observability over debugging
- Infrastructure as code

The *what* stays the same. The *how* changes completely.

## Post 6
Here's the reassuring part:

Work orders, assets, PM, workflow, security groups -- all the same conceptually. Your Maximo domain expertise transfers 100%.

It's the technology layer underneath that shifts. And honestly? The new tools are better. They're just unfamiliar.
