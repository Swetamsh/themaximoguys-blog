# Corrective Action Program & Clearance/Tagout

> 🎯 **Who this is for:** Regulatory/QA and operations leads who own the Corrective Action Program and the safety controls around work — and who need to know exactly which Maximo Nuclear applications carry the CAP loop and the nuclear tagout discipline.

**Series:** Part 5 of 7 — *Maximo for Nuclear on MAS 9* | **Read time:** 18 minutes

---

## Two Things a Nuclear Plant Cannot Get Wrong

There are two disciplines a nuclear plant is judged on constantly: how it *learns from problems* and how it *controls work safely*. The first is the Corrective Action Program. The second is the clearance-and-permit stack. Maximo Nuclear carries both, and this part walks each.

They are related in practice. A poorly controlled job creates a condition; the CAP captures and corrects it; the lessons feed operating experience that shapes the next job's controls. Maximo holds that loop in named applications — with one honest "no named app" note on OPEX that we will name directly.

---

## Condition Reports as the CAP Engine

The **Corrective Action Program (CAP)** is the backbone of nuclear quality assurance, and its regulatory anchor is **10 CFR 50 Appendix B XVI (Corrective Action)**, backed by **NQA-1**. In Maximo Nuclear, the CAP engine is a single, purpose-built application: **Condition Reports (Nuc)**.

Condition Reports (Nuc) documents facility non-conformances and drives the full CAP loop. From the source, the application links to:

- **Evaluation** of the reported condition
- **Significance classification** — how serious the condition is
- **Apparent-cause and root-cause** analysis
- **Corrective actions**, tracked as follow-up work orders

That last point is the design decision that makes it work. Corrective actions are not free-floating to-do items; they are **tracked follow-up work orders**. The fix inherits the entire work-management lifecycle — planning, assignment, status, execution, and records — and it stays connected to the condition report that spawned it.

<aside>

💡 **Key insight:** The reason Condition Reports (Nuc) is a genuine, named application — not a configuration pattern — is that the CAP is *the* App B XVI obligation, and it has a defined loop: identify, evaluate, classify significance, determine cause, correct, and verify effectiveness. That loop is exactly what a regulator or an INPO evaluation examines. A purpose-built CAP engine, with corrective actions as tracked work orders, is what turns "we have a corrective action program" from an assertion into an auditable, evidenced process.

</aside>

The full CAP loop, as it runs in Maximo:

1. A **condition report** is raised for a non-conformance.
2. The condition is **evaluated** and its **significance classified**.
3. **Apparent-cause or root-cause** analysis is performed as significance dictates.
4. **Corrective actions** are created as follow-up work orders and executed under the work-management lifecycle.
5. Effectiveness is reviewed — closing the loop back to the original condition.

For the regulatory lead, this is one of the two or three places an auditor looks first, and the answer is clean: a named CAP engine under App B XVI, with corrective actions living as tracked work orders.

## OPEX and the Solutions Library

Operating experience — OPEX — is how the industry avoids repeating each other's failures. The natural question is whether Maximo ships an OPEX application. Here is the honest answer, consistent with the rest of the series.

**OPEX is a partial capability with no named app.** It is implemented via **Condition Reports (Nuc) plus a reusable Solutions library plus Commitment Tracking (Nuc)**. The INPO Operating Experience program is a voluntary framework, and Maximo supports it through this combination rather than a dedicated OPEX module.

The **Solutions library** is the practical heart of it: a reusable store of known problems and their resolutions, so that when a familiar condition recurs, the captured solution can be found and applied. Paired with Condition Reports for capture and Commitment Tracking for follow-through, that is a workable operating-experience mechanism.

<aside>

💡 **Key insight:** OPEX is a "no named app" capability, so name it as one. "Operating experience is supported through Condition Reports, the Solutions library, and Commitment Tracking" is accurate. "Maximo has an OPEX module" is not. The Solutions library is real and useful — it is the reuse mechanism — but the OPEX *program* is assembled from cooperating applications and a voluntary INPO framework, not shipped as a single app. Scope it as configuration and reuse, and you have set the expectation correctly.

</aside>

**Commitment Tracking (Nuc)** earns its own mention: it ensures regulatory commitments are carried by work orders through to close-out. When a condition report or an event generates a commitment — to a regulator, or internally — Commitment Tracking is what keeps it from being lost, tying it to the work that discharges it.

## The Clearance and Permit Stack

Now the safety controls around work. A nuclear plant's isolation and tagout discipline is more rigorous than a typical industrial site's, and Maximo Nuclear reflects that with a dedicated stack of applications rather than a single Lockout/Tagout screen.

| Application | Role |
|---|---|
| **Clearances (Nuc)** | Nuclear tagout / isolation prior to work or surveillance — richer than core Maximo Lockout/Tagout |
| **Clearances Kiosk (Nuc)** | Shop-floor kiosk for tag apply and restore |
| **Clearance Groups (Nuc)** | Shared tags across related clearances |
| **Sign On/Off (Nuc)** | Personnel sign-on and sign-off against the work |
| **Permits (Nuc)** | Streamlined permit issuance for common work types |

The lead application is **Clearances (Nuc)**, and the source is explicit that it is **richer than core Maximo Lockout/Tagout.** It handles nuclear tagout and isolation prior to work or surveillance — the controlled process of placing a system or component in a safe, isolated state before anyone works on it. The reason it is richer is the nuclear discipline: isolation for a safety-related surveillance or a work package on safety-related equipment carries requirements that a generic LOTO screen does not encode.

The supporting applications complete the model. **Clearances Kiosk** brings tag apply-and-restore to the shop floor, where the work actually happens. **Clearance Groups** lets shared tags span related clearances — important when multiple jobs share an isolation boundary. And **Sign On/Off** tracks the personnel signing onto and off of the cleared work.

<aside>

💡 **Key insight:** The reason nuclear tagout is a *stack* rather than a single app is that the discipline has multiple moving parts — the clearance itself, the physical tag apply/restore at the point of work, shared tags across related jobs, and personnel sign-on. A single Lockout/Tagout screen collapses all of that. The nuclear stack keeps them distinct because each is separately auditable, and because a shared isolation boundary across two jobs is a real, dangerous edge case that Clearance Groups exists to handle correctly.

</aside>

## Permits: Wrapping Controls Around the Work

The last piece is **Permits (Nuc)**, which provides streamlined permit issuance for common work types — and the source notes permit types such as **heat-stress** permits, linked to clearances. There is also a **Quick Permits (Nuc)** application for streamlined issuance of common work-type permits.

Permits close the loop between the clearance stack and the work package built in Part 3. A nuclear work package does not just carry a job plan and a schedule; it carries its **safety controls** — the clearance that isolates the equipment, the permit that authorizes the specific work conditions, and the sign-on that records who is on the job. All of it travels with the work in the system of record.

<aside>

💡 **Key insight:** Put Parts 3 and 5 together and you have the full anatomy of a nuclear work package: the work order (Part 3), the operability check via Impact Plans (Part 3), the clearance and isolation (this part), the permit and its conditions (this part), and personnel sign-on (this part). A generic EAM gives you the work order. The nuclear solution gives you the work order *plus the controls that make it safe to execute* — each as a named, auditable application, with only OPEX riding the configuration-and-reuse pattern.

</aside>

## How the CAP and the Controls Connect

Step back and the two halves of this part are one loop:

1. Work is controlled with **Clearances (Nuc)**, **Permits (Nuc)**, and **Sign On/Off** — the isolation and authorization that keep execution safe.
2. When something goes wrong — a non-conformance, an event — a **Condition Report (Nuc)** captures it.
3. The CAP evaluates, classifies significance, determines cause, and creates **corrective actions as follow-up work orders** under App B XVI.
4. The lessons are captured in the **Solutions library** and carried by **Commitment Tracking**, feeding operating experience back into how the next job is controlled.

That loop — control the work, capture the problem, correct it, learn from it — is the operational heart of nuclear quality assurance, and Maximo Nuclear carries every stage of it in named applications, with OPEX honestly flagged as a configuration-and-reuse pattern.

## Key Takeaways

- **Condition Reports (Nuc)** is the CAP engine under **10 CFR 50 Appendix B XVI** — non-conformance, evaluation, significance classification, cause analysis, and corrective action.
- **Corrective actions are tracked as follow-up work orders**, keeping the fix inside the work-management lifecycle and tied to its condition report.
- **OPEX has no named app** — it is Condition Reports plus a reusable **Solutions library** plus **Commitment Tracking**; name it as configuration and reuse.
- **Clearances (Nuc)** is **richer than core Lockout/Tagout**, supported by Clearances Kiosk, Clearance Groups, and Sign On/Off.
- **Permits (Nuc)** and Sign On/Off wrap safety controls around the nuclear work package, so the clearance, permit, and sign-on travel with the work.
