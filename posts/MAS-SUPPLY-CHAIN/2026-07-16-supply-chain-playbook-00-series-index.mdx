# MAS 9 Supply-Chain Playbook: Storekeeper & Buyer Quick Wins

> **Who this is for:** The two people who actually run supply chain in MAS 9 — the storekeeper at the bin and the buyer at the desk — plus the supervisors, leads, and admins who support them and want a role-organized field guide instead of a feature catalog.

**Estimated read time for this index:** 8 minutes | **Total series read time: ~4.5 hours across 10 parts**

---

## Why This Series Exists

Most MAS 9 supply-chain writing is organized the way IBM builds the product: by feature. Here is the inventory model, here are the nine contract types, here is the receiving engine, here is the AI stack. That is exactly the right way to understand *what the platform can do*, and we wrote it that way ourselves — the **MAS FEATURES series** covers supply-chain core and inventory (Part 21), procurement, contracts and receiving (Part 22), mobile supply chain (Part 23), and the AI supply-chain pipeline (Part 24). If you want the architecture, go there.

But a feature catalog is not what a storekeeper needs at 7 a.m. on go-live morning, staring at an empty Start Center with forty-five apps in the "Go To" menu and no idea which three of them run the storeroom. And it is not what a buyer needs when the migration dumped three thousand requisitions into their queue and the inbox is full of stale approvals nobody triaged.

Those two people don't need to know *what a Purchase Contract is*. They need to know **what to click, what to query, and what rhythm to keep** so the day runs clean. This series is written for them — organized by role and by task, grounded in the same MAS 9.0 and 9.1 behavior the feature guides describe, but pointed at the desk, not the diagram.

It answers the questions the feature guides skip:

*"Where does my work actually live now, and how do I get it on one screen?"*

*"Whatever happened to Issue Current Item?"*

*"How do I receive a 30-line PO without 30 separate clicks?"*

*"How do I get from an approved requisition to an issued purchase order in under two minutes?"*

*"The vendor is two weeks late — does MAS chase them, or do I?"*

Every part is a set of quick wins, config recipes, checklists, and daily rhythms. Nothing here needs custom Java. Almost nothing needs a paid add-on — and where one genuinely changes the game, we say so and mark it optional.

## Where This Series Fits

This is deliberately a **role-and-task playbook**, not a feature course. Here is the division of labor so you never read the same thing twice:

| If you want… | Read… |

Put simply: MAS FEATURES tells you *what MAS 9 supply chain is*. This series tells you *how to run it as a storekeeper or a buyer* — the Start Center you deploy in the first hour, the saved queries that kill the empty filter bar, the receiving pass that clears a truck, the reservation hygiene that stops phantom stockouts, the PR-to-PO conversion that takes two minutes, the RFQ template that turns a two-hour setup into fifteen minutes, and the escalation engine that chases late vendors for you.

## Key Themes Across the Series

**Day 1 is a configuration decision, not a training problem.** The storekeeper and the buyer both succeed or fail on whether their Start Center is pre-loaded before they log in. Tier 0 — templates, saved queries, KPIs — is the highest-leverage hour of the whole rollout.

**One app, one document, one audit trail.** INVUSAGE for the storeroom and the compressed Create-PO action for the buyer both follow the same principle: fewer transactions, more lines per document, a cleaner trail. Learn to work in batches and the day gets faster.

**Clean data is the whole game.** Reservation hygiene, reorder points, and vendor master cleanliness are not housekeeping — they are the load-bearing foundation. Auto-reorder, escalation, and scorecarding are only as trustworthy as the data underneath them.

**Config beats code.** The escalation engine, the approval thresholds, the match tolerances, the RFQ templates — all of it is admin-app configuration, Comm Templates, and SQL conditions. A well-run MAS 9 supply chain writes almost no Java.

**Know the honest gap.** Procurement has no role-based application yet — buyers stay in the classic apps. We name that plainly in Part 10 so it doesn't surprise you on go-live day.
