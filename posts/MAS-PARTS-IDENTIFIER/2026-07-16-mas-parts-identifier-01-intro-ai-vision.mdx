# Intro & the AI Vision Behind Maximo Parts Identifier

> 🎯 **Who this is for:** Maintenance leads, storeroom and inventory managers, and Maximo administrators weighing whether the Parts Identifier add-on earns a place in their MAS 9 estate — and who want the honest version, not the demo version.

**Series:** Part 1 of 4 — *Maximo Parts Identifier on MAS 9* | **Read time:** 19 minutes

---

## 📖 The Problem: The Part With No Name

Every asset-intensive operation has the same quiet, recurring loss, and almost nobody measures it. A technician is mid-repair. They pull a component — a bearing, a seal, a solenoid, a proximity switch, a hydraulic cartridge valve — and it has no readable identity. The stamped part number wore off years ago. The label is a smear of hydraulic oil and sun damage. The equipment is a decade-old skid from a vendor who was acquired twice and no longer answers the phone. The technician holds a part in their hand and cannot tell you what it *is*, which means they cannot tell you what to *order*.

What happens next is the expensive part. They walk it to the storeroom and hope the storekeeper recognizes it. They text a retired colleague, dig through vendor binders, guess at a cross-reference, order the wrong thing, and discover the mistake three days later when the wrong box arrives and the asset is still down. Multiply that across a large plant, a receiving dock that takes deliveries without paperwork, and a workforce where a third of the crew has under two years of tenure, and you have a genuine, unglamorous drain on wrench time.

This is the exact, narrow moment Maximo Parts Identifier is built for. Not inventory management at large — you already have that. Not procurement — you already have that too. Just this: *the part in the hand has no name, and someone needs the name in the next two minutes.*

<aside>
💡 **Key insight:** Parts Identifier is not a platform, it is a moment. It targets one specific failure — "I am holding a part I cannot identify" — and everything about how you evaluate, deploy, and tune it should stay anchored to how often, and how expensively, that moment actually happens in your operation.
</aside>

## 🎯 What Maximo Parts Identifier Actually Is

Stated plainly, and grounded in IBM's own description: **Maximo Parts Identifier uses AI-powered visual recognition to help field technicians identify unknown parts.** When a technician encounters a part they cannot identify — no visible part number, a worn label, or unfamiliar equipment — they take a photo, and the AI identifies the part, cross-references it against the inventory catalog, and provides ordering information.

That is the whole promise, and it is worth admiring how small and sharp it is. It does not try to be a maintenance system, a catalog manager, or a procurement engine. It inserts one AI-assisted step — *photo in, identity out* — at the precise point where a human would otherwise stall. The identity it returns is not a novelty; it is a live pointer back into the systems you already run: the **item master**, the **inventory** balances, the **storeroom** locations, and the **purchasing** flow.

Think of it as a translator that sits between the physical world and your Maximo catalog. A technician speaks "unknown metal object" and Parts Identifier answers in your own item numbers. Everything downstream — the reservation, the issue, the purchase requisition, the reorder — runs on the rails Maximo already has. The AI's only job is to get you from a photograph to a candidate `ITEMNUM`, at which point normal Maximo takes over.

## 📦 The Six Features, Read Honestly

IBM documents six capabilities. Here they are, alongside what each one really means once you get past the demo and into an operation.

| Feature (as documented) | What it does | What it means in practice |
|---|---|---|
| **Visual parts identification** | Point the camera at a part, the AI identifies it | The headline. Quality depends entirely on the catalog it was trained on and the photo it is given — garbage in, garbage out applies hard |
| **Parts catalog integration** | Cross-references identified parts with the Manage item master | The reason it is useful and not a toy. A match is a real `ITEMNUM`, not a floating label |
| **Inventory lookup** | Shows current stock levels and storeroom locations | Turns "what is this" into "do we have one, and where" in the same tap — often the more valuable half |
| **Ordering integration** | Initiate a purchase requisition from the identification result | Closes the loop: identify → confirm → requisition without re-keying |
| **Similar parts suggestion** | Suggests alternative or substitute parts | Quietly powerful for MRO, where a functional substitute often beats waiting for the exact SKU |
| **Historical recognition** | Learns from confirmed identifications over time | The flywheel. Every human confirmation is a labeled example that should make the next match better |

Read that table twice, because it contains the whole strategy. The first feature is the one people talk about, but the second and third are the ones that create value: an identification that lands on a real item number and immediately tells you whether it is on the shelf is worth far more than a clever guess that leaves you to go find out. And the last feature — historical recognition — is the one that determines whether the system gets *better* or merely *stays the same* under your specific parts, lighting, and cameras.

<aside>
💡 **Key insight:** The value is not in the guess; it is in what the guess is connected to. A vision model that says "this looks like a 6205 bearing" is a party trick. A model whose answer is a live `ITEMNUM` with three in stock at storeroom CENTRAL and a one-tap requisition is an operational tool. Parts Identifier is the second thing — but only if your item master and inventory are in good shape, which is a theme we return to in Part 3.
</aside>

## 🤖 How Visual Recognition Actually Works

IBM does not publish the internals of the model, and you should be suspicious of anyone who claims to know them in detail. But the *general* mechanics of visual part recognition are well understood, and understanding them is the difference between using this tool wisely and being disappointed by it. What follows is standard computer-vision domain knowledge, not a description of IBM's specific implementation.

### From pixels to candidates

A photograph is, to a computer, a grid of numbers. A recognition model's job is to boil that grid down into a compact numerical summary — call it a **feature vector** or **embedding** — that captures the *visually meaningful* content (shape, proportions, texture, distinctive markings) while ignoring the noise (background, lighting, angle). During training, the model learns embeddings for a catalog of known parts. At inference time, it computes an embedding for the technician's photo and finds the catalog entries whose embeddings are closest. Those nearest neighbors become the **candidate list**, each with a similarity score.

The documented flow matches this exactly: step one, the technician photographs the unknown part; step two, the AI model analyzes the image and matches it against the trained parts catalog; step three, results come back — part number, description, manufacturer, storeroom quantity, and an order link. The magic word in step two is *matches* — it compares, it does not read.

### Why "confidence" is not "correctness"

Every candidate comes with a score, usually presented as a confidence percentage. This number is routinely misread, so be precise about it: **confidence measures how similar the photo looked to something in the catalog — not the probability that the answer is right.** A model can be 95% confident and wrong (two different SKUs that look nearly identical) and it can be 60% confident and right (a correct match photographed in poor light). Treat the score as a *ranking and triage* signal, not a *truth* signal.

| Confidence band | Reasonable interpretation | Suggested technician action |
|---|---|---|
| **High (top candidate clearly ahead)** | Strong visual match to one catalog entry | Verify one or two distinguishing details, then confirm |
| **Medium (several close candidates)** | Visually ambiguous — likely a family of similar parts | Compare the top 3 against physical detail; use dimensions/markings to break the tie |
| **Low (no candidate stands out)** | Part may be outside the trained catalog, or the photo is poor | Retake with better framing/lighting; if still low, fall back to a human or manual lookup |

That middle row is where the real world lives, and it is why the human stays in the loop. Parts Identifier is a *decision-support* tool: it narrows a haystack to three needles and lets a person make the final call.

### The catalog is the model's vocabulary

This is the single most important thing to internalize. **A recognition model can only ever return things it was trained on.** If a part is not represented in the trained catalog, the best the model can do is return a wrong-but-plausible neighbor with a low score. The catalog is the model's entire vocabulary of the world; anything outside it is, by definition, unspeakable. That fact drives the whole of Part 4 (training and accuracy tuning): the fastest way to make the tool better is almost never "a better model" and almost always "a better, broader, cleaner set of labeled example images of *your* parts."

<aside>
⚠️ **Watch out:** Do not describe the tool to end users as "AI that knows what parts are." It knows what parts *look like*, relative to a catalog you helped build. Set that expectation on day one and you will avoid the two classic failure modes: technicians trusting a confident wrong answer, and technicians abandoning the tool the first time it fumbles a part it was never taught.
</aside>

## 🧠 Why This Is Genuinely Hard for Industrial Parts

Consumer visual search — point your phone at a landmark or a houseplant — feels magical because those categories are visually distinct and abundantly photographed. Industrial MRO is the hostile opposite, and being honest about why keeps your expectations calibrated.

- **Near-identical SKUs.** A storeroom can hold twenty bearings that differ only in bore diameter by fractions of a millimeter, or ten O-rings that differ only in durometer. To a camera at arm's length, they are the same object. Vision alone frequently *cannot* separate them; the tie is broken by dimensions, markings, or context the human still has to supply.
- **The long tail.** A large plant may carry tens of thousands of active items. Getting good coverage across a catalog that large — with enough example images per item — is a real data undertaking, which is exactly why IBM lists collecting sample images as a distinct, time-boxed task.
- **Field conditions.** Grime, corrosion, paint, glare, cramped mounting positions, and one-handed phone photography all degrade the input. The same part looks different caked in coolant than it does clean on a bench.
- **Wear changes appearance.** The very condition that makes a part unidentifiable to a human — a worn, damaged, or corroded surface — also makes it harder for a model trained mostly on clean examples.

None of this makes the tool useless. It makes it *bounded*. The realistic win is not "photograph anything, get a perfect answer." It is "for the subset of parts we trained well, cut a five-minute identification stall down to twenty seconds, and get the technician a stock check and an order link in the same motion." That is a good, if narrow, win — and narrow, high-value is precisely the shape of this app.

## 🔄 A Worked Example: The Unlabeled Bearing

Let us ground all of this in one concrete story we will carry through the series. Marcus is a maintenance technician at a mid-sized packaging plant. A conveyor gearbox is running hot, and he finds a failed roller bearing pressed onto the output shaft. The bearing's markings are worn to ghosts; there is no work order line item for it because the last person to touch this gearbox left the company.

1. **The stall (old world).** Without Parts Identifier, Marcus's next 30 minutes are a scavenger hunt — storeroom, phone photos to a WhatsApp group, a vendor PDF, a hopeful guess. The gearbox stays down while he plays detective.
2. **The capture (new world).** Marcus opens Parts Identifier, wipes the bearing face, and photographs it square-on against a neutral background with good light. (Part 2 is entirely about doing this step well.)
3. **The candidates.** The AI returns three: a `6205-2RS` sealed bearing at high confidence, a `6205-ZZ` shielded variant close behind, and a `6305` at a distance. The top two are visually near-twins — this is the "medium confidence, break the tie" situation from the table above.
4. **The human tiebreak.** Marcus checks the seal type: the removed bearing has rubber contact seals, not metal shields. That confirms `6205-2RS`. The model narrowed twenty possibilities to two; Marcus's eyes closed the last gap in five seconds.
5. **The lookup.** The result is a live `ITEMNUM` — say `BRG-6205-2RS` — and Parts Identifier shows **3 on hand at storeroom CENTRAL, bin B-14**. He does not need to order anything; he needs to walk fifty feet.
6. **The loop closes.** Had stock been zero, the order link would have started a purchase requisition pre-filled with the identified item (Part 3). Either way, Marcus confirms the match, and that confirmation becomes a labeled training example that should sharpen the next `6205` identification (Part 4).

Total identification time: under a minute, most of it spent taking a good photo. That is the promise made concrete — and every step depends on things *outside* the AI: a clean item master, accurate inventory, and a well-chosen set of training images.

## 🏷️ Where It Fits — and Where It Doesn't

IBM lists five use cases. They share a common shape: a person who does not (yet) hold the tribal knowledge to name a part on sight.

| Documented use case | Why the tool helps here |
|---|---|
| **Field technicians** on unfamiliar equipment | The core case — new asset, no history, no readable ID |
| **Receiving clerks** on parts that arrive without documentation | Undocumented deliveries become catalog-able instead of "shelf of mystery boxes" |
| **Inventory staff** cataloging unlabeled stock | Turns a wall of unknowns into item-master candidates |
| **Apprentice technicians** learning parts identification | A patient tutor that pairs a photo with a name and a location |
| **Emergency repairs** when subject-matter experts are unavailable | The 2 a.m. breakdown when the one person who knows is asleep |

Just as important is where it does **not** fit — the anti-use-cases, so you deploy it for the right reasons:

- **Parts with a readable identity.** If there is a legible part number, barcode, QR, or RFID tag, use that. It is faster and exact. Vision is for the residual unknown, not a replacement for scanning.
- **Precision that only measurement can give.** If the distinction between two candidates is a tolerance or a material grade the eye can't see, the model can't either. That is a caliper-and-spec job, not a camera job.
- **Catalog-less shops.** If your item master is thin or filthy, the tool has nothing to match against and nothing to point to. Fix the catalog first (Part 3), or the AI is matching against noise.

<aside>
💡 **Key insight:** The best predictor of whether Parts Identifier will earn its keep is not the sophistication of the AI — it is the state of your item master and the frequency of genuinely unidentifiable parts. Strong catalog plus real identification pain equals value. Weak catalog or rare pain equals a science project.
</aside>

## ⚠️ The Honest Positioning: A Niche App, and IBM Says So

Here is the posture the rest of this series takes, and it is IBM's own. In the suite roadmap, Parts Identifier is rated **low-to-medium value** with a **niche use case and limited user base**, its deployment complexity is **low**, its only hard prerequisite is **MAS deployed**, and it is slotted into a **late deployment phase** — explicitly behind Health, Monitor, Predict, Visual Inspection, AI Assist, and Optimizer. The recommended priority is candid: **evaluate later.** The suggested evaluation is a small team (one to two people) over roughly **22–44 hours — about a week** — to decide whether it fits at all.

This is not a knock on the app; it is the right frame for it. A capability that solves a real but narrow problem, deploys simply, and touches a small group of users *should* be approached as a scoped evaluation rather than a flagship program. The wrong move is to treat it like Health or Predict and build a business case as if it will transform operations. The right move is to run a tight pilot, measure whether it actually saves identification time on *your* parts, and let that number decide.

<aside>
⚠️ **Watch out:** Because AppPoints are a shared pool, it is tempting to "just turn it on because we have the points." Resist deploying it with no owner and no success metric. An unowned AI feature that nobody validated becomes shelfware that quietly erodes trust in the whole suite. Give the pilot a name, a metric, and a decision date.
</aside>

## 🔧 Practical Notes: How to Approach the Evaluation

If you have decided the problem is real enough to look at, here is how to point the week well — a preview of the deployment and tuning we detail in Part 4.

1. **Quantify the pain first.** Before any AI, count it: how many work orders per month stall on part identification, and for how long? If you cannot find the pain, you have your answer already.
2. **Pick a bounded parts domain.** Do not try to cover the whole item master. Choose one high-value, high-confusion family — bearings, seals, sensors, hydraulic cartridges — where many SKUs look alike and mistakes are costly.
3. **Interview the field team.** The people who live the stall know which parts cause it. Their list is your training-image priority list.
4. **Collect sample images deliberately.** IBM calls this out as its own task for a reason — it is the real work. Photograph your chosen family across realistic conditions (clean and dirty, multiple angles, mounted and loose).
5. **Test on *known* parts.** Measure accuracy against parts whose identity you already know, so you have ground truth. A demo on cherry-picked items proves nothing; a test on a representative sample tells you whether to proceed.
6. **Decide with a number.** End the week with a metric — identification success rate on your representative sample, and estimated time saved per event — and let that, not enthusiasm, drive the go/no-go.

The rest of this series follows that arc: Part 2 makes the capture step reliable, Part 3 connects the match to your catalog and inventory, and Part 4 turns a one-week evaluation into a tuned, trustworthy pilot.

## Key Takeaways

- **Parts Identifier is a moment, not a platform** — it targets the single failure of holding a part you cannot name, and pipes the answer back into your existing item master, inventory, and purchasing.
- **The six features form a chain:** identify → catalog-match → stock lookup → order → suggest substitutes → learn from confirmations. The value lives in the middle links, not the flashy first one.
- **Visual recognition is pattern matching against a trained catalog** — it does not read part numbers and does not understand parts, so confidence is a ranking signal, not a proof of correctness.
- **Industrial parts are genuinely hard** for vision — near-identical SKUs, a long tail, and messy field conditions bound the realistic win to "narrow but fast."
- **IBM positions it as niche and evaluate-later** — the correct response is a tight, metric-driven pilot on a bounded parts family, not a flagship rollout.
