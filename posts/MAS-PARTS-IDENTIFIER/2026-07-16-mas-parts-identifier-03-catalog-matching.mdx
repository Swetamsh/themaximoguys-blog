# Catalog Matching & Inventory Integration in Maximo Parts Identifier

> 🎯 **Who this is for:** Storeroom and inventory managers, master-data owners, and Maximo administrators who will connect Parts Identifier to the catalog, inventory, and purchasing they already run — and who know that the catalog is where this succeeds or fails.

**Series:** Part 3 of 4 — *Maximo Parts Identifier on MAS 9* | **Read time:** 21 minutes

---

## 📖 The Match Is Only Useful If It Lands on a Real Item

Parts 1 and 2 got us from a part in the hand to a ranked list of candidates. This part is about the thing that makes that list *matter*: cross-referencing the match into the systems you already run. IBM's description is precise about this — the tool cross-references identified parts with the **Manage item master**, shows current **stock levels and storeroom locations**, and lets the technician **initiate a purchase requisition** from the identification result.

Sit with why that chain is the whole value proposition. A vision model that returns "this looks like a 6205 bearing" is trivia. A vision model whose answer is item `BRG-6205-2RS`, which is **stocked three-deep in storeroom CENTRAL, bin B-14**, and which can become a purchase requisition with one tap when it isn't — that is an operational tool. The AI produces a *candidate*; the item master, inventory, and purchasing modules produce the *outcome*. Parts Identifier is best understood as a new front door onto your existing catalog, not a new catalog.

That framing has a hard consequence, which is the spine of this entire part: **if the catalog behind the door is a mess, the door opens onto a mess.** Everything below follows from that.

<aside>
💡 **Key insight:** Parts Identifier does not create data — it *lands on* data. The item master is simultaneously the model's vocabulary (what it can recognize) and the match's destination (what it hands back). Both roles mean the same thing: the quality ceiling of the tool is the quality of your catalog.
</aside>

## 🗂️ The Maximo Objects Behind the Match

Nothing here is new Maximo. The tool rides on the same objects you have administered for years; it just enters them from an image instead of a search box.

| Concept | Maximo object | What it holds | Role in the Parts Identifier flow |
|---|---|---|---|
| Catalog item | `ITEM` | The item master record — number, description, classification, specs | The destination of every match; the model's vocabulary |
| Item specifications | Classification + attributes | Structured specs (size, material, rating) via `CLASSSTRUCTUREID` | Disambiguates look-alikes; powers substitute logic |
| Inventory balance | `INVENTORY` / balances | On-hand, reserved, available by storeroom and bin | The "do we have it, and where" answer |
| Storeroom | `LOCATIONS` (type storeroom) | Physical stocking location | Where the technician walks to, per result |
| Purchase requisition | `PR` / `PRLINE` | The demand record the order link creates | Turns "we're out" into a procurement signal |
| Substitute/alternate | Alternate/substitute items | Functionally interchangeable items | Powers the similar-parts suggestion |

The mental model: the AI narrows to a candidate `ITEMNUM`; that key unlocks the item's `INVENTORY` rows to answer availability; if availability is zero, the order link writes a `PR`; and the item's classification and substitute relationships feed the similar-parts suggestions. Each hop is ordinary Maximo. The novelty is only the entry point.

## 🧹 Garbage Catalog, Garbage Match: Item-Master Data Quality

Here is the uncomfortable truth of MRO: most item masters are not clean. They accreted over decades, through mergers, migrations, and hurried one-off adds during breakdowns. Parts Identifier does not paper over that — it *exposes* it, because a vision match against a duplicated, thinly described, unclassified catalog produces ambiguous, low-confidence, or plain wrong results. Fixing the catalog is usually the single highest-value thing you can do before (or during) a pilot.

### Duplicate and near-duplicate items

The classic MRO disease: the same physical bearing exists as `BRG-6205-2RS`, `BEARING 6205 2RS`, and `6205-2RS-SKF` because three people created it in three sites over ten years. For a human, these are annoying. For a vision model, they are corrosive: the *same* photo can match *different* item numbers depending on which duplicate happened to have better training images, so identical parts get inconsistent answers and your inventory picture fractures across phantom SKUs. Deduplication — collapsing these into one canonical item — is the most impactful cleanup you can do, and it pays off far beyond this tool.

### Thin or inconsistent descriptions

`"BEARING"` is not a description; it is a shrug. When descriptions are terse or non-standard, three things break: the human reading the candidate list can't confirm the match, substitute logic has nothing to reason over, and search fallbacks (when the AI can't help) return junk. A disciplined noun-modifier convention — *what it is, then its distinguishing attributes* (`BEARING, BALL, 6205, 2RS, 25MM BORE`) — makes candidates human-verifiable and dramatically improves every downstream feature.

### Classification and commodity structure

Classification (`CLASSSTRUCTUREID`) and commodity structure are what let Maximo reason about a part as a *member of a family* rather than an isolated string. Well-classified items — bearings under a bearings class with attributes for bore, seal type, and series — give both the human and the substitute engine the structured detail that separates look-alikes. A flat, unclassified catalog throws that information away and leaves the eye to do all the work.

<aside>
⚠️ **Watch out:** It is tempting to deploy the AI and hope it compensates for a dirty catalog. It cannot, and it will make the dirt *visible* in a new and public way — technicians will see the same part return different item numbers and lose trust fast. Sequence it correctly: clean the target parts family *first*, then point the tool at it. A pilot on a clean, deduplicated family will succeed where a pilot on the raw catalog will embarrass you.
</aside>

## 📊 Inventory Lookup: From "What Is It" to "Do We Have It"

The identification is often not even the most valuable output — the *stock answer* is. The moment a match lands on `BRG-6205-2RS`, the tool surfaces its inventory position: on-hand quantity, storeroom, and bin. That collapses two separate errands — identify, then go find out if we stock it — into one motion.

A few realities to design around:

- **Multi-storeroom truth.** Availability is per storeroom. A part can be zero at the plant storeroom and three at central stores. Surface the location, not just a global number, so the technician walks to the right shelf.
- **Available ≠ on-hand.** On-hand includes reserved stock. The number that matters for a technician deciding "can I take one now" is *available* (on-hand minus reservations). Make sure the crew understands which number they're reading.
- **Balances must be real.** If cycle counts are stale, the tool will confidently send a technician to an empty bin. The match was right; the balance lied. This is an inventory-accuracy problem the tool inherits, not one it causes — but it *surfaces* it, which is another reason accurate balances matter more once this is live.

<aside>
💡 **Key insight:** For a large share of events, the win isn't "the AI named the part" — it's "the AI named the part *and told me it's on the shelf fifty feet away*." Identification plus instant availability is a bigger time-saver than identification alone, which is exactly why catalog-plus-inventory integration is the heart of the app rather than a nice-to-have.
</aside>

## 🛒 Ordering Integration: Identify → Requisition

When stock is zero, the order link closes the loop by initiating a purchase requisition pre-populated from the identified item. Crucially, this does not bypass your controls — the `PR` enters the *normal* Maximo purchasing flow (approval, sourcing to `PO`, receipt). Parts Identifier removes the re-keying between identification and requisition; it does not remove procurement governance.

### The zero-stock worked example

Rejoin Marcus, but change one fact: the gearbox bearing comes back as `BRG-6205-2RS` with **0 available** at every storeroom.

1. **Match and confirm.** Marcus verifies the seal type, confirms `BRG-6205-2RS`. Stock reads zero.
2. **Tap to requisition.** He uses the order link. Maximo drafts a purchase requisition — say `PR1187` — with a line for `BRG-6205-2RS`, quantity and default sourcing pulled from the item record.
3. **Context carries.** Because the identification happened against a work order, the requisition can reference the originating work (`WO`)/asset, so procurement sees *why* the part is needed and can prioritize a line that's holding an asset down.
4. **Normal flow takes over.** `PR1187` routes for approval, gets sourced to a vendor, becomes a `PO`, and is received against the storeroom. None of that is new; the only thing Parts Identifier did was eliminate the twenty-minute gap where Marcus would otherwise have hunted for the right item number to requisition.
5. **The record is clean.** Because the requisition landed on a *canonical, deduplicated* item, it reinforces good data instead of spawning yet another one-off "BEARING" line — provided you did the Part 3 cleanup.

That step 5 is the quiet dividend: a well-run Parts Identifier deployment doesn't just consume good catalog data, it *encourages* it, because every requisition flows through the canonical item rather than around it.

<aside>
⚠️ **Watch out:** Do not let the order link become a bypass around sourcing rules or approved-vendor lists. The requisition is a demand signal that must obey the same approvals and sourcing controls as any other PR. Configure it to respect your purchasing policy, and audit early requisitions to confirm it does.
</aside>

## 🔁 Similar Parts and Substitutes: The Quiet MRO Win

The similar-parts suggestion is easy to overlook and genuinely useful. In MRO, the exact SKU being out of stock does not always mean the asset waits — a *functionally equivalent* substitute already carried elsewhere can keep it running. Surfacing "the exact part is out, but this interchangeable alternate is stocked at central stores" turns a lead-time delay into a fifty-foot walk.

But interchangeability is a serious word, and this is where discipline matters:

- **A substitute must be genuinely interchangeable** for the application — same fit, form, and function, and any rating the application depends on. A bearing with the right dimensions but a lower speed rating is not a valid substitute on a high-speed shaft.
- **Interchangeability is an engineering decision, not an AI guess.** The model can *suggest* visual or catalog neighbors; a human (reliability or engineering) must *bless* the substitution relationship in the item data. Curated substitute/alternate-item relationships in the item master are what make the suggestion trustworthy.
- **Over-suggesting is a risk.** A list of loosely "similar-looking" parts that aren't actually interchangeable invites the wrong install. Prefer a short list of *engineered* substitutes over a long list of look-alikes.

Done right, this feature is one of the better arguments for the app in a large MRO shop: it converts stockouts into substitutions and shaves real downtime — but only on top of curated substitute data, which is, once again, a catalog investment rather than an AI feature.

## ⚠️ Edge Cases and Troubleshooting

The failures here are almost all *data* failures wearing an AI costume. Diagnose accordingly.

| Symptom | What it means | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| Same photo returns different item numbers | Duplicate items in the master | Deduplicate to a canonical item; retrain images onto it |
| Match is right but "0 on hand" is wrong | Stale inventory balance | Cycle-count the bin; fix the balance — the vision was correct |
| Candidate list is unreadable to the technician | Thin/non-standard descriptions | Apply a noun-modifier description standard to the family |
| Substitute suggestion caused a wrong install | Substitutes weren't truly interchangeable | Curate substitute relationships with engineering sign-off; prune look-alike-only entries |
| Requisition bypassed approvals/sourcing | Order link not bound to purchasing policy | Configure PR creation to enter the standard approval/sourcing flow; audit early PRs |
| Right part, wrong site's stock shown | Storeroom/site scoping confusion | Ensure results scope to the technician's site/storerooms; surface location per result |
| Good match, but part isn't in inventory at all | Item exists in master but is not stocked | Expected — offer the order path; consider whether it *should* be stocked |
| Tool "can't find" a common part | Item missing from master entirely | Add the item to the master and to the training set (Part 4) |

The recurring diagnosis: when Parts Identifier disappoints at this stage, walk the data before you blame the model. Nine times in ten it is a duplicate, a stale balance, a thin description, or a missing item — all fixable, all worth fixing regardless of the AI.

## 🧠 Why Catalog Quality Determines Everything

Step back and the logic is airtight. The model can only recognize items that exist, cleanly, in the catalog it was trained on. The match is only actionable because it resolves to a canonical item with real inventory behind it. The substitute suggestion is only safe because a human curated interchangeability into the item data. The requisition is only clean because it lands on a deduplicated item. Every single one of those depends on the item master, not the AI.

This is why a Parts Identifier project is, in truth, half an *item-master project*. That is not a detour from the goal — it *is* the goal's foundation, and the cleanup you do pays dividends across inventory accuracy, purchasing, reporting, and reliability long after the pilot's go/no-go. If leadership asks "what do we get even if we don't keep Parts Identifier," the honest and attractive answer is: a materially cleaner catalog. Frame the investment that way and it is easy to justify even for a niche app.

## 🔧 Practical Notes Before You Roll This Out

- **Clean the target family first.** Deduplicate, standardize descriptions, and classify the parts family you'll pilot on *before* pointing the tool at it. This is the highest-leverage prep work, full stop.
- **Fix inventory accuracy where you'll pilot.** Cycle-count the storerooms in scope so "on hand" is trustworthy — a right match plus a wrong balance still sends a technician to an empty bin.
- **Curate substitutes with engineering.** Populate substitute/alternate relationships deliberately, with sign-off, and keep the list tight and genuinely interchangeable.
- **Bind the order link to policy.** Make requisitions from results obey your normal approvals and sourcing; audit the first batch.
- **Scope results to the technician's site.** Surface per-storeroom availability and location, not a global number, so the walk is to the right shelf.
- **Measure the data dividend.** Track duplicates collapsed and descriptions standardized as a first-class benefit of the project, independent of the AI's hit rate.

## Key Takeaways

- **The match matters only because it lands on a real item number** — Parts Identifier is a front door onto the item master, inventory, and purchasing you already run.
- **Three objects carry the flow:** `ITEM` (the catalog and the model's vocabulary), `INVENTORY` (availability by storeroom and bin), and `PR` (the requisition the order link creates).
- **Item-master data quality is the ceiling** — deduplication, standardized descriptions, and classification decide whether matching is consistent and results are actionable.
- **Inventory lookup is often the bigger win than identification itself** — "what is it *and* is it on the shelf" beats "what is it" alone.
- **Substitute suggestions are a real MRO win** — but interchangeability is a curated engineering decision, not an AI guess, and the project is half an item-master project either way.
